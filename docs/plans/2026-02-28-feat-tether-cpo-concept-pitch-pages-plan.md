## Problem Statement / Motivation

Tether needs to convert CPOs who are skeptical, time-poor, and overwhelmed by the complexity of grid balancing and e-credit markets. The core challenge isn't building a calculator — it's building **trust in a number**. Existing competitor tools (PowerFlex, Sympower, Voltus) produce opaque outputs that CPOs can't verify, feeding the skepticism that already exists toward startup revenue claims in a market with high-profile failures.

The two concepts attack this trust problem from different angles: Concept A through radical transparency, Concept B through temporal narrative that makes the estimate feel grounded in real market dynamics rather than a fabricated projection.

### Research Insights

**Strategic framing:** The compelling narrative is not any single revenue stream in isolation. FCR-D averages ~EUR 112/charger/year — not transformative alone. The story is the convergence of three simultaneous forces in 2025-2026: (a) Nordic mFRR market reform creating new 15-minute flexibility markets, (b) Sweden's Reduktionsplikt now accepting electricity credits with 4x RED III multiplier, and (c) most CPOs running negative EBITDA at 5-10% utilization. A tool that helps CPOs visualize stacked revenue across all three addresses both timing and pain point.

**Competitive white space confirmed:** FLEXECHARGE has proven the VPP/FCR case for large DC networks. Jedlix/Kraken dominates residential smart charging for utilities. Virta is beginning to add flexibility features to its CPO platform. The gap is a **CPO-facing estimation tool** that sits upstream of aggregation platforms — the "what's my upside?" calculator that helps CPOs understand potential before committing to a partner.

## Detailed Execution Plan

### Phase 1: Content Foundation

Before writing either pitch page, establish the shared foundation that both concepts build on.

#### Task 1.1: Draft Problem Statements

Each concept needs its own problem framing — NOT a restatement of Tether's brief ("How could we show CPOs their potential revenue?"). The problem must be framed from the CPO's perspective.

**Concept A problem angle:** CPOs evaluating energy service partners see opaque revenue claims they cannot independently verify. When every vendor shows a different number with no methodology, the rational response is to trust none of them. The result: promising partnerships stall in the sales pipeline because CPOs can't build an internal business case around a number they can't explain to their own board.

**Concept B problem angle:** CPOs make decisions based on annual lump-sum revenue projections — static numbers that feel abstract and disconnected from their operational reality. A single annual figure ("EUR 300K") provides no insight into when revenue starts, how it grows, or what market dynamics drive it. This abstraction makes it easy to defer action indefinitely, because the cost of delay is invisible.

Both framings dig beneath the brief to a root cause (trust deficit / abstraction problem) rather than restating it.

##### Research Insights: Problem Statement Best Practices

**The "Why?" ladder technique:** Strong problem statements result from asking "Why?" 3-5 times. Example:
- *Why does Tether need a lead magnet?* → CPOs don't convert
- *Why don't CPOs convert?* → They don't trust the revenue projections
- *Why don't they trust them?* → No one shows the methodology behind the numbers
- *Why does that matter?* → CPOs can't build an internal business case around an unverifiable number

The final "Why?" level is the problem statement. This is what judges and clients recognize as genuine root-cause thinking, not brief restatement.

**The micro-scenario technique:** A 40-50 word vignette of a real CPO's experience communicates more than abstract analysis. Example for Concept A: *"When a CPO operations manager receives three different annual revenue projections from three different flexibility partners — each claiming to use 'market data' — none of those numbers reaches the board meeting. The business case never gets built."*

**Use concrete numbers in the problem.** "64-80% charger reliability" and "single-digit utilization (~8%)" ground the problem in verifiable reality, signaling that your team did actual research.

#### Task 1.2: Curate Research & Insights (3-5 per concept, distinct)

Each concept's insights must specifically motivate that concept's design choices. Overlap should be minimal.

**Concept A — Trust & Transparency Insights:**

1. **CPO skepticism is data-driven, not irrational.** The EV charging industry has seen exits and bankruptcies. Charger reliability rates sit at 64-80% (gridX European EV Charging Report 2025). CPOs have been burned before. *Implication: trust must be earned through verifiable evidence, not marketing.*

2. **No existing CPO revenue tool shows its methodology.** Competitive analysis of PowerFlex, Sympower, Voltus, Stable Auto, and GridBeyond found that all produce opaque outputs. None allow CPOs to see formulas, data sources, or assumptions. *Implication: transparency is an open competitive gap.*

3. **Interactive B2B calculators convert 2x better than static content.** Interactive tools achieve 8.3% conversion vs. 3.8% for gated PDFs, with 4:27 average dwell time vs. 1:24 (B2B calculator benchmark data). *Implication: the format itself drives business results.*

4. **No tool combines e-credits + flexibility revenue.** Every existing calculator shows one revenue stream. Tether's dual-stream model is unique, and the combined number is materially higher than either stream alone. *Implication: showing both creates a differentiated and more compelling value proposition.*

5. **Real market data exists and is publicly citable.** FCR/mFRR prices from Mimer (Svenska kraftnat), RES-E% from Green Grid Compass, and CO2 market prices are all publicly accessible. The 4x RED III multiplier is EU regulation. *Implication: transparency is feasible — the data is available to be shown.*

**Concept B — Temporal Narrative & Loss Aversion Insights:**

1. **Charts improve memorability by 65% over numbers alone.** Visual data presentation is retained significantly longer than text-based numbers (data visualization research). *Implication: a timeline chart will stick in a CPO's mind longer than "EUR 305K/year."*

2. **Loss aversion is 2x stronger than gain motivation.** Behavioral economics research (Kahneman & Tversky) shows people are roughly twice as motivated to avoid a loss as to achieve an equivalent gain. *Implication: framing revenue as "left on the table" is psychologically more compelling than "available to earn."*

3. **Grid balancing prices show strong seasonal variation.** Actual 2025 Mimer data shows winter price spikes (heating demand drives grid imbalance) and summer troughs. Revenue is NOT a flat line. *Implication: showing seasonal patterns proves the estimate is grounded in real market dynamics, not fabricated.*

4. **Sweden's mFRR EAM (launched March 2025) created extreme price volatility.** The Nordic 15-minute energy activation market immediately produced imbalance prices hitting EUR 2,000/MWh during peaks. *Implication: the revenue opportunity is real, recent, and growing — a timeline makes this trend visible.*

5. **CPOs need shareable artifacts for internal decision-making.** CPO purchasing decisions involve multiple stakeholders (CEO, CFO, board). A shareable "revenue story" snapshot travels to internal decision-makers who never interact with the tool directly. *Implication: shareability extends the tool's reach beyond the initial user.*

##### Research Insights: Strengthening the Insights Sections

**Updated market data points to incorporate:**
- FCR-D Up pricing in Sweden averaged ~EUR 112/charger/year for 11kW chargers at 15% utilization in 2025
- mFRR EAM imbalance prices reached EUR 2,000/MWh during peaks immediately after the March 2025 launch
- Sweden's Reduktionsplikt was raised from 6% to 10% in July 2025, and now explicitly accepts electricity credits (elkrediter) — proposed credit value of 0.27 SEK per publicly charged kWh
- Revenue stacking across day-ahead + FCR markets yields 76% higher profit than single-market participation
- Most public DC charging networks report 5-10% average utilization, with most CPOs running negative EBITDA
- EY/Eurelectric 2025 study: EV owners save EUR 450-2,900/year through smart + bidirectional charging; EUR 4 billion in annual savings for European grid operators from EV flexibility

**The "insight + implication" pattern:** Every insight should follow the formula: *[Data point with source] → [So what? Why does this matter for THIS concept?]*. This pattern is what separates a research dump from a research-informed argument. The plan already uses this pattern — keep it.

**Concept A additional data point:** The "verifiable claim" effect. Research shows that offering the *option* to verify builds trust, even though most users never actually verify. For a revenue estimator, this means the cost of transparency is minimal (adding a methodology panel and source links) while the trust benefit is substantial. This is the strongest single argument for Concept A's transparency approach.

**Concept B additional nuance on loss aversion:** In B2B procurement contexts, loss aversion has been validated — procurement officers rate cost-of-inaction scenarios as more decision-relevant than gain scenarios. However, there is a risk: if oversold, loss framing can feel manipulative. The mitigation is to ground the "revenue left on the table" framing in factual seasonal data (real Mimer prices, real months), so the urgency feels data-driven rather than manufactured.

| **Innovation** | 2 | 4 |

**Rationale notes:**

- Concept B's Feasibility score of 3 reflects higher technical complexity (animated charting, scenario logic, shareable output generation) while still being achievable with client-side JS and no backend
- Both score 5 on Impact because they fill a genuine market gap (combined revenue streams) and directly solve the client's conversion challenge

##### Research Insights: Scorecard Presentation

**Honesty advantage in academic evaluation:** Unlike investor pitches where you sell confidence, academic pitches reward demonstrated thinking. A scorecard with a 3/5 on feasibility accompanied by a thoughtful rationale will score higher than a 5/5 with no explanation. The existing scores are well-calibrated for this.

**Rationale phrasing tip:** Use "because" clauses, not just adjectives. Instead of "High feasibility," write "5/5 feasibility because all data and formulas already exist in Tether's materials, and the tool can be built as a client-side web page with zero backend dependency."

**Visual contrast matters:** When the scorecard is viewed side-by-side across both concepts, the different profiles (Concept A: 5-2-5, Concept B: 5-4-3) make trade-offs immediately visible. This is exactly what the I2P guide wants — don't homogenize the scores.

##### Research Insights: Concept A Enhancements

**Tagline refinement:** Research suggests taglines should lead with the outcome (what the CPO gets) rather than the mechanism (how the tool works). Current tagline focuses on mechanism ("shows its work"). Consider alternatives:
- Outcome-first: *"See exactly what your chargers could earn — and check every number yourself."*
- Trust-first: *"The only EV revenue estimator that lets you verify every assumption."*
- Combined: *"Your chargers' untapped revenue, calculated transparently with real market data."*

The mechanism ("shows its work") can be the subhead or opening sentence of The Concept section instead.

**Progressive disclosure best practices:**
- **Level 1 (default):** Hero number (total annual revenue, CPO's 40% share) + per-charger figure + donut/bar showing split between e-credits and flexibility. This is what the sales team shows in demos.
- **Level 2 (one click):** Revenue formula shown with labeled variables. Each variable links to its source. Assumptions stated explicitly (utilization rate, flexibility potential, market prices).
- **Level 3 (optional deep dive):** Full methodology with actual monthly market price averages, RES-E% data, and links to Mimer/Green Grid Compass where CPOs can verify independently.

**The "verifiable claim" trust effect:** Research shows that offering the *option* to verify builds trust, even though most users (estimated 80-90%) never actually click through to the source data. The option itself is the trust signal. This means the methodology layer doesn't need to be perfectly designed — its existence is what matters.

**Per-charger revenue anchoring:** Including a per-charger figure (e.g., "~EUR 112/charger/year from flexibility alone") makes the estimate tangible and testable. A CPO with 500 chargers can do the mental math instantly (500 x EUR 112 = EUR 56K). This is more persuasive than only showing a total.

**Input design research:** Optimal B2B calculator inputs are 3-5 fields. Below 3, the estimate feels generic. Above 5, abandonment spikes. The planned 3-4 inputs (charger count, power, country/region, public vs. residential) are well-calibrated. Consider making charger power default to 11kW (the most common AC charger) so the CPO only *changes* it if they have a different configuration.

##### Research Insights: Concept B Enhancements

**Loss aversion in B2B — validation and risks:**
- Loss aversion has been validated in B2B procurement contexts. Procurement officers rate cost-of-inaction scenarios as more decision-relevant than gain scenarios.
- However, there is a backlash risk: loss framing that feels manufactured or manipulative reduces trust rather than building it. The mitigation is to ground the "revenue left on the table" counter in factual, verifiable seasonal data (real Mimer prices, real months), so the urgency feels data-driven rather than pressure selling.
- Recommended approach: present the loss aversion counter as a *secondary* element, not the hero. The hero should be the growing revenue itself. The "missed revenue" counter should be a quieter number that CPOs notice, not a flashing alarm.

**Temporal visualization research:**
- No direct A/B test exists comparing month-over-month vs. annual lump-sum in B2B sales tools specifically.
- However, converging evidence supports the approach: (a) hyperbolic discounting research shows near-term concrete amounts motivate more than distant abstract totals, (b) the endowed progress effect means showing early months with small but real revenue creates momentum, and (c) temporal unfolding increases perceived effort and therefore perceived accuracy ("they modeled every month, not just threw out an annual number").
- The strongest argument for the timeline format is not the animation itself — it's the seasonal variation it reveals. When revenue lines rise in winter and dip in summer, the estimate demonstrates awareness of real market dynamics.

**Diagram guidance for static PDF (avoiding wireframe territory):**
- A simple conceptual diagram showing 3 diverging lines over a time axis is the safest visual. Labels should be verb phrases describing what happens, not UI element names.
- Use smooth curves (not jagged data lines) to signal "this is conceptual, not a data chart."
- Label lines directly on the chart (no legend box — legends look like UI mockups).
- Use rounded numbers (EUR 50K, 100K, 200K) rather than precise figures.
- Include a small disclaimer: *"Illustrative projection based on industry benchmarks"*
- Do NOT include axis labels like "Month" or "EUR" in a grid format — this crosses into chart mockup territory. Instead, annotate the diagram conversationally: *"After 12 months..."*, *"By month 24, the gap between 'with Tether' and 'without' represents..."*

**Writing interactive experiences in text (the micro-scenario approach):**
Instead of describing features ("the user can toggle scenarios"), describe a moment:

*"A CPO operations manager enters 500 chargers at 11kW. In five seconds, she watches a revenue line climb from zero to EUR 56K by month 12 — then toggles on e-credits and sees the line jump to EUR 112K. The gap between the two lines? That's the revenue she's already leaving on the table."*

This 50-word vignette communicates the concept more effectively than a 200-word feature description. Use present tense and active verbs. Name a role ("operations manager"), not "the user."

**Shareability format recommendation:**
- For internal champion forwarding, a PNG snapshot image travels best (easily pasted into emails, Slack, presentations)
- Include: the timeline chart with the CPO's specific numbers, a one-sentence summary, Tether logo, and a CTA URL
- The snapshot should look like a "result" not a "screenshot" — design it as a self-contained card

##### Research Insights: PDF Design Best Practices

**Recommended tool: Canva.** Fastest path to a polished result for students. Use a one-pager or infographic template as the starting skeleton, then customize.

**Visual hierarchy for one-page concept pitch:**
1. **Header band** — Concept name (large, bold), tagline (smaller), team + client logo, date. Use Tether's brand color if available.
2. **Problem section** — Bold opening sentence. Keep to 2-3 sentences. Can use a subtle background color to set it apart.
3. **Research & Insights** — Numbered list format for scannability. Each insight: bold claim + 1-sentence support + source in parentheses.
4. **The Concept** — This is the longest section. Use subheads (What/Who/How/Different) to break it up. Include the conceptual diagram here if applicable (Concept B).
5. **Why This Matters** — 1-2 bridging sentences + scorecard table. Keep the table clean and compact.
6. **Next Steps** — Numbered list, 4 items maximum.
7. **Appendix** — Separate page or section at the bottom. Organized by sub-headings with labeled links.

**Whitespace target:** 30-40% of the page should be white space. Resist the temptation to fill every corner — a pitch that says one thing clearly beats one that says five things vaguely.

**Typography:** Use no more than 2 fonts. One for headings (bold, larger), one for body text. 11-12pt body text minimum for readability. Use bold and color sparingly to highlight key numbers (EUR figures, percentages).

**Hero number technique:** Consider making the headline revenue number (e.g., "EUR 305K/year") visually prominent — large font, centered, colored. This is the number the reader should remember. Everything else supports it.

#### Task 3.4: Address Key Risks Identified in SpecFlow Analysis

These items surfaced during specification analysis and must be handled:

1. **"Why two concepts instead of one?"** — Each page is standalone per I2P rules. The student team is required to present multiple concepts. No framing needed within the pages themselves; this is handled at the team presentation level.

2. **Conveying interactive tools in static PDF** — Use vivid descriptive text and simple diagrams (allowed by I2P guide). Concept B is higher risk here — prioritize clear textual description of the animated experience. Use the micro-scenario technique (see Task 2.2 Research Insights). Consider a simple diagram showing 3 diverging revenue lines over a time axis.

3. **Currency handling** — Use EUR consistently. Tether operates in Nordics; EUR is the standard for Nordic energy markets (Mimer prices are in EUR/MW).

4. **Markets beyond Sweden** — The tool is "adaptable to other countries over time" per the brief. Mention this in Next Steps but don't overcomplicate the concepts. Sweden is the primary market and the one with the most data.

5. **40% revenue share** — This is Tether's standard split (confirmed in their materials). State it clearly and transparently. If it varies by contract, that's a detail for the sales conversation, not the lead-magnet tool.

##### Research Insights: Additional Risk Mitigations

6. **Word budget overflow risk (highest risk).** The Research & Insights section is the most likely to exceed its ~200 word budget. Mitigation: each insight should be exactly 2 sentences — one bold claim, one supporting sentence with source. No exceptions. Push extended analysis to the Appendix.

7. **"5-second test" for headers/taglines.** Evaluators comparing multiple student concepts side by side in a single session will experience concept fatigue. They will default to remembering the concept with the clearest name, the most striking hero number, and the most honest self-assessment. Run a mental "5-second test": if someone sees only the header for 5 seconds, do they know what the concept does?

8. **Concept B diagram risk.** The line between "concept diagram" and "wireframe" is subjective. Safe zone: smooth curves with text annotations. Danger zone: labeled axes, grid lines, or anything that looks like a data chart. Label the diagram as "Illustrative — conceptual revenue projection."
